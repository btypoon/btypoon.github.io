In this project, I tried to wrote a simulation on analysing the evolution of Spin Glass in Ising model.

Definition of Spin Glass

According to wiki, spin glass defines a disordered magnetic state of a material. Comparing to ferromagnetic materials, where their atomic magnetic spins all align in the same direction, spin glass material possess random magnetic spins and couplings.

The word "glass" was used as analogy between the atomic magnetic disorder property of spin glass to the atomic positional disorder property of conventional glasses.

In conventional glass, their atomic bond structures are highly irregular and amorphous. Materials exhibiting similar properties with no observed long-range periodicity are called amorphous, non-crystalline and sometime also described as "glassy".

Therefore, "Spin glass" does not refer to your typical conventional window glasses.

Ising model

In Ising model, we consider a lattice space where local structure repeats periodically in all direction. Let there be $N$ points in this lattice space. We assign each point in the lattice a magnetic dipole moment $s$ of value from one of two states, up and down. One way to represent it was to use $0$ and $1$. Here, we follow the convention of using $\pm1$.

\begin{align*}s_k\in\{-1,+1\}\end{align*} \begin{align*}k\in\{1,2,...,N\}\end{align*}

For simplicity, we only consider 2D square lattices of fixed dimension $m\times n$

\begin{align*}m\times n = N\end{align*}

Coupling constant $J_{ij}$

$J_{ij}$ represent the interation between $s_i$ and $s_j$

  • If $J_{ij}\gt 0$, the interation is called ferromagnetic.
  • If $J_{ij}\lt 0$, the interation is called antiferromagnetic.
  • If $J_{ij}\lt 0$, the spins are noninteracting

We can think of them as friends and enemies. Spins whose are friends, with positive interaction $J\gt 0$, want to align with each other. Spins whose are enemies, with negative interaction $J\lt 0$, want to opposite each other.

Also, notice the symmetry when we speak of the interation between $s_i$ and $s_j$.

\begin{align*}J_{ij}=J_{ji}\end{align*}

Energy function

In a given state of a system with known $J_ij$, $s_i$ and $s_j$, the total energy, hamoltonian or sometime called "cost function" or "discomfort function" of the system can be represented by

\begin{align*}E(s_1,s_2,...,s_N)=-\sum_{i\lt j}{J_{ij}s_is_j} \end{align*}
where this equation allow infinite range of interaction for distant $i$ and $j$.

We are interested in minimizing the total energy of the system.

Simplifying the coupling interaction $J$

In real world, the interation $J_{ij}$ takes on a random normally distributed value with zero mean. However, We can simplify the problem by letting $J_{ij}$ be either $1$ or $-1$ to represent positive and negative interaction respectively.

\begin{align*}J_{ij}\in{\{-1,1\}}\end{align*}

Moreover, in the real world, there exist interation between distant spins. But, we would like to simplify the problem by restricting the range of interation to the neighborhood only.

i.e.\begin{align*}J_{ij}&=0 &\text{ for } |i-j|\ne 1\\J_{ij}&=\{-1,1\} &\text{ for } |i-j|= 1\end{align*}

Frustration

In some situation, no assignment of signs for a spin would minimize the total energy of a system. We call this a frustrated system.

For example, in a triangularly arranged configuration of antiferromagnetic spins. All spins are enemies and want to opposite one another.

i.e.\begin{align*}J_{12}=J_{23}=J_{31}=-1\end{align*}
The total energy is the same no matter what sign we give the last spin.

We also call this system to be sixfold degenerate.
